Photographic Prints

A photographic image is formed through the exposure of a photosensitive emulsion to light: this is the essence of a photograph. Photo emulsion is composed of a light-sensitive image material (e.g. silver particles) dispersed in a binder substance (e.g. gelatin). The emulsion is carried on a paper or sometimes a plastic support. Photo prints may be monochrome or full-color, and are typically positive images. Monochrome prints are split into three categories: 1-layer (image material only), 2-layer (image material in a binder), or 3-layer (image material in a binder atop a baryta coating).

Salt Print

Synonyms
  • Salted paper print
Dates
1839–1860s
Surface Sheen
Matte
Paper Fibers
Clearly visible
Image Tone
Reddish brown (untoned), purplish brown (toned); may appear yellowish brown or greenish yellow if faded.
Description
A salted paper print is a single-layer print comprised of an uncoated paper support with a silver image deposited in the paper fibers. A positive print is created by placing a negative in contact with the sensitized silver printing-out paper and exposing it to sunlight. Paper fibers will be clearly visible. Many silver salt print images appear to be embedded into the paper surface. The surface sheen will likely be matte (exhibiting no surface gloss) and its texture rough due to the absence of a binder layer. If unmounted, the paper will be semi-translucent when viewed over a light source and may in some cases bear a watermark (Stulik & Kaplan, 2013). The image will likely range from light brown to reddish brown (untoned) to purplish brown (toned). If the print is faded the image may appear yellowish brown. Typically found mounted, in albums, or loose.
Composition
Support Image
Paper (uncoated) Silver
Deterioration
Salted paper prints are sensitive to pollutants, humidity, and light. Images are often faded and may show pale yellow at their edges. Highlight details will fade first, but overall fading is also common. Untoned images will be especially vulnerable to fading. "Foxing," other types of paper staining, and embrittled supports are also common. If a salt print was retouched, it will now likely appear as a dark spot and may be mistaken as a form of deterioration (Reilly, 1986).
Risk Level
High. A tendency toward image fading and discoloration will be greatly accelerated by environmental factors such as pollutants (inherent and airborne), high humidity, and light exposure. If a salt print is untoned, it is of higher priority.
Common Size(s)
Varied; Image dimensions correspond to the process negative
Background
Salted paper prints were commonly produced from 1839 to the 1860s. They were the earliest positive print format and the primary positive process for contact-printing directly from paper negatives and glass negatives (until the 1850s). Exposure times ranged from a few minutes to a few hours, requiring that the image be visually inspected throughout the process to determine when the exposure was complete. Before fixing and washing, the image could be toned with gold chloride to increase permanence and create a richer tone. The comparatively less arduous silver albumen print overtook the silver salt process in the mid-1950s, just before the salt print faded into obscurity in the 1860s.

Cyanotype

Synonyms
  • Blueprint
  • Ferro-prussiate print
Dates
1880s–1950s
Surface Sheen
Matte
Image Tone
Brilliant Prussian blue
Description
A cyanotype is a single-layer print comprised of a blue pigment image embedded in an uncoated paper’s fibers. The image color is a brilliant Prussian blue with continuous tone. Image is typically of poor contrast and sharpness. The paper fibers are clearly visible and paper texture will likely be rough. Surface finish is extremely matte with no surface gloss due to the absence of a binder layer. Typically found in albums or loose, the process was used to produce snapshots or blueprints.
Composition
Support Image
Paper (uncoated) Blue pigment
Deterioration
Cyanotypes are sensitive to light—will fade quickly in direct, intense light—but are otherwise quite stable if kept in dark storage. Furthermore, faded cyanotypes can regain substantial image density when kept in dark storage. As with other early photographic processes, the paper support will often be the weakest link. Paper staining, discoloration, and embrittlement are common deteriorative traits.
Risk Level
Moderately low. Cyanotypes are relatively stable and image permanent if kept in dark storage conditions and out of an alkaline pH environment. The integrity of the support paper is often the decisive factor in the long term preservation of cyanotypes.
Common Size(s)
Varied; Image dimensions correspond to the process negative
Background
Cyanotypes were produced from the 1880s to 1950s. The cyanotype process was invented in 1842 but was not widely used as a photographic print material until the 1880s; popularity waned in the 1910s. Cyanotypes were most often created by the amateur because of their affordability and simple processing. The cyanotype process has commonly been used to create photograms. The term "blueprint" generally refers to line drawings reproduced by the cyanotype process for schematic or technical drawings.

Platinum/Palladium Print

Synonyms
  • Platinotype
  • Palladiotype
Dates
1873–1930s
Surface Sheen
Matte
Image Tone
Neutral gray-black; or warm brown, brown-black, or purple/blue-black (depending on toning)
Description
A platinum/palladium print is a single-layer print comprised of a platinum or palladium image embedded in uncoated paper’s fibers. The surface is extremely matte with no surface gloss due to the absence of a binder layer. The paper fibers are visible and the image exhibits no fading or silvering. Images can be characterized as rich and soft, with a mid-tone density value ranging from silver-gray to black, though warmer tones (often browns) were achievable through the use of additives/toning, from warm brown to sepia to brown-black, and some cases purple/blue-black. Typically found mounted, framed, in albums, or loose.
Composition
Deterioration
Platinum and palladium are the most long-lasting of all chemical photo processes. The metal image itself is permanent and will exhibit no fading, but the paper support will face a heightened risk of yellowing and embrittlement due to the image’s inherent acidity. The support will have little flexibility; avoid handling prints for this reason. Deterioration of the paper support will often shift the image toward a warmer tone (evident in highlights). If the image surface has been stored in contact with another piece of paper, there may be signs of catalytic degradation in the form of a mirror image "burned" into the facing paper. This staining is irreversible—do not house platinum prints in direct contact with other photographic materials.
Risk Level
Low. The integrity of the support paper is the decisive factor in long term preservation; platinum and palladium images are among the most permanent photographic image materials yet pose an enormous threat to its paper support. Do not allow unnecessary handling and consider creating access copies.
Common Size(s)
Varied; Image dimensions correspond to the process negative
Background
Platinum prints were produced from 1873 to the late 1930s. They were expensive to produce and therefore were typically used for high-end portraiture and fine art photography. Inflation of platinum prices near the turn of the century gave rise to the palladium print, or palladiotype, which simply substitutes metal palladium for platinum. Palladium prints lack the tonal range of platinum prints, but are otherwise analogous, chemically and visually.

Albumen Print

Dates
1850–c. 1895
Surface Sheen
Semi-glossy to glossy
Image Tone
Purplish brown to chocolate brown; light yellow brown (fading) common.
Description
An albumen print is a two-layer print comprised of a paper support with an albumen binder and silver image. Prints were generally produced on smooth-surfaced thin paper. Depending on the period, burnishing and rolling techniques may or may not have been implemented to smooth after mounting. Therefore the surface sheen may range from semi-glossy (1850–c. 1870) to glossy (c. 1870–1895) (Reilly, 1980). Paper fibers are clearly visible. The image tone is commonly purplish brown but may vary from warm chocolate brown to yellowish brown (when faded). Prints after 1855 were always toned with gold chloride. Papers after 1860 may have been tinted with a pink or blue dye in order to mask the natural yellowing of the albumen layer. Typically found mounted, framed, in albums, or loose. The print’s thin paper support is usually found on a cardstock mount: a carte-de-visite (2½" × 4¼" ), cabinet card (6¼" × 4" ), or stereocard (about 3½" × 7" ).
Composition
Deterioration
Albumen prints will likely exhibit yellowing and some fading. Most albumen prints exhibit distinct yellow or yellowish brown staining in the non-image and highlight areas (Reilly, 1980). As the albumen binder tends to yellow, overall print discoloration follows. Highlights tend to lose image contrast and detail first. A cracked or crazed pattern may be seen in dark areas, particularly in the case of a thin emulsion coating or an unburnished print. If the print is unmounted from its cardstock mount it will curl up very tightly. Some albumen prints will suffer a form of paper staining known as foxing. Foxing is characterized by the appearance of reddish brown blotches, and will often occur in patches. If an albumen print was retouched, and it will now likely appear as a dark spot and may be mistaken as a form of deterioration (Reilly, 1986).
Risk Level
Moderately high. Albumen prints are sensitive to light, pollutants, and humidity. High humidity and excessive light exposure will accelerate fading and the deterioration of the albumen binder. High humidity may also cause foxing to occur. Low humidity will cause albumen to crack and delaminate. Folds, creases, and general mishandling will also cause cracking and fissuring.
Common Size(s)
Typically mounted on cards. 2½" × 4" –4¼" (carte-de-visite); 4¼" × 6½" (cabinet card); 3–4½" × 7" (stereocard)
Background
Albumen prints were produced from 1850 to 1895. Albumen prints were the dominant print medium for commercial photography between 1860 and the 1890s, replacing daguerreotypes as the prevailing portrait medium. Albumen carte-de-visite portraits were incredibly popular, allowing people to collect photographs of their family members and prominent public figures alike. Albumen was gradually superseded by gelatin and collodion printing-out papers around 1885.

Carbon Print

Analogous
  • Monochrome carbro print
Dates
1850–1950s; popular from 1870–1910
Surface Sheen
Differential; dark areas of image appear glossy while highlights appear semi-matte
Image Tone
Varies; neutral black, reddish brown, or chocolate brown.
Description
A carbon print is a two-layer print comprised of a paper support with a carbon black pigment image carried by gelatin. The surface exhibits differential sheen, with dark areas of the image appearing more glossy than semi-matte highlights. Thicker application of the pigment/gelatin is relative to the density of the image and will lend toward a subtle relief effect. Carbon images are recognizable by their dense, dark tones, either neutral black, reddish brown, or chocolate brown, but any pigment color is possible. Carbon prints may be printed on a variety of colored papers. The paper fibers are visible in highlights. The print may be labeled "Permanent" or "Autotype." A carbon print and a monochrome carbro print will be indistinguishable; though different processes, each possess the same key elements and a similar image stability. Typically found loose, mounted, framed, or in albums, and may be quite large in size.
Composition
Support Binder Image
Paper Gelatin Carbon black pigment
Deterioration
Carbon images will exhibit little to no fading or yellowing. Carbon black is among the most light stable of pigments. Darker areas of the image may develop large cracks, where the gelatin layer is thickest. As with all prints on paper, they are susceptible to mechanical damage and staining. When found unmounted, carbon prints will have a tendency to curl in on themselves.
Risk Level
Low. As carbon prints contain pigment rather than silver or other unstable components, the integrity of the support paper is the decisive factor in the object’s permanence, as is their display and storage history.
Common Size(s)
Varied; Image dimensions correspond to the process negative
Background
Carbon prints were produced from 1855 to the 1950s, and were popular between 1870 and 1910. Because of their rich image quality, carbon prints were often used for portraiture, fine art, and fine art reproduction. Though understood as a stable and rich alternative to silver processes, carbon prints never reached a wide audience, largely due to the time and effort demanded.

Glossy Collodion Print

Synonyms
  • Collodion POP print
  • Collodion printing-out paper
  • collodion-chloride
Dates
1880–1910
Surface Sheen
Semi-glossy to glossy; may exhibit iridescent effect when viewed under fluorescent lights.
Paper Fibers
Not visible
Image Tone
Purple-brown (toned) to reddish-brown (untoned, faded)
Description
A glossy collodion print is a three-layer print comprised of a paper support with a baryta layer, collodion binder, and a silver image. The surface finish ranges from semi-glossy to glossy due to the thick baryta layer. Baryta layer may be tinted, exhibiting an overall pink, blue, or green tint. A collodion print may also have a glazed surface, resulting in a high-gloss finish. Most glossy collodion prints exhibit a subtle iridescent effect on their surface when viewed under fluorescent lighting. Image tones range from reddish-brown to purple or violet brown. Paper fibers will not be visible. A collodion print may be difficult to distinguish from the gelatin POP; however, it is not often critical to make this distinction. Typically found mounted, framed, in albums, or loose; collodion prints were often produced as cabinet cards.
Composition
Deterioration
Collodion prints exhibit very little image fading. Collodion prints are extremely sensitive to surface abrasion, resulting in white scratch marks where the image material has been removed and the baryta layer is exposed. This is a distinctive trait of collodion print deterioration. The collodion layer may also delaminate or exhibit tiny cracks due to fluctuating relative humidity and/or mishandling.
Risk Level
Moderate. Care must always be shown when handling collodion prints to avoid abrasion, scratching, and surface wear. Do not attempt to clean print surface. A stable environment should also prevent the emulsion from delaminating.

Background
Collodion prints were produced from 1880 to 1910, and were conventionally used for portraiture, landscape and architecture photography during that time. Glossy collodion papers were the first emulsion type printing-out-paper (POP) to be marketed. Due to the public’s favor for glossy commercial portraiture at the time, collodion POP’s popularity rose quickly in the 1880s, but fell second to the more versatile gelatin POP in the following decade.

Matte Collodion Print

Synonyms
  • Matte collodion POP print
  • Matte collodion printing-out paper
Dates
1895–1910
Surface Sheen
Semi-matte with slight luster
Paper Fibers
Not visible
Image Tone
Neutral gray-black; brownish black, purplish black
Description
A matte collodion print is a three-layer print comprised of a paper support with a baryta layer, collodion binder, and a silver image. Owing to a thinner baryta layer than its glossy collodion counterpart, the surface will have a semi-matte quality with only a slight luster. Matte collodion images should be assumed toned and will range in tone from neutral gray-black to a near neutral brownish black to purplish black, and will not appear faded due to the common use of stabilizing toners. Matte collodion prints were most often toned with gold and platinum, giving them an appearance similar to platinum prints. Matte collodion are often in the form of cabinet cards or are mounted in a paper folder.
Composition
Deterioration
Due to a strong reliance on gold and/or platinum image toning, matte collodion print images are exceptionally stable. Typically matte collodion have greater stability than the glossy collodion print, exhibiting less fade and silver mirroring, but will slightly fade and yellow over time. As with all collodion prints, they are sensitive to surface abrasion and may exhibit white scratch marks where the image material has been removed and the baryta layer is exposed. The collodion layer may also delaminate or exhibit tiny cracks due to fluctuating relative humidity and/or mishandling.

Moderate. Care must always be shown when handling collodion prints to avoid abrasion, scratching, and surface wear. Do not attempt to clean print surface. Stable humidity should also prevent the emulsion from delaminating.

Background
Matte collodion prints were produced from 1895 to 1910. Their commercial introduction in the 1890s, and their divergence from the earlier collodion POP print, was based on the public’s preference for both platinum prints and matte surfaced prints in general. They were meant to mimic the appearance of platinum prints; both were reliant on image toning but matte collodion POP prints were cheaper to produce. Matte collodion paper was the material of choice among portrait photographers until 1910, when more convenient silver gelatin developing out papers emerged.

About the Process
Although dye-transfer prints are considered photographic images, they are not the result of direct exposure to light. Each color layer is transferred to the print surface by bringing the matrix film in contact with the paper surface.

If applied unevenly, uncoated areas may develop yellowing, loss of detail, and increased sensitivity to abrasion. The white border found around instant photos should never be cut off or removed; it provides a barrier to atmospheric pollutants and removal would also weaken the structural integrity of the photograph. Unmounted prints may curl.
Risk Level
Moderate. The absolute uniqueness of the item/image should factor in an instant print’s value appraisal and preservation. Preservation risk largely depends on whether the image was produced according to the film specifications (e.g. whether processing chemicals have been neutralized, whether a protective coating was correctly applied), and the type of film (peel-apart, coated peel-apart, or integral). On the whole, B&W instant photos are more stable than color instant photos, but as with all silver-based photographs, they are susceptible to light fading and oxidative deterioration.

Background
B&W instant photos were produced from 1947 through the late 2000s. The instant photographic process was developed by Edwin Land, one of the cofounders of the Polaroid Corporation, who sought to create a photographic process that did not require a darkroom. He believed there would be a large market for photographs that could be exposed and developed immediately. Polaroid was the first to manufacture instant photographic film and cameras, with the release of the Land Camera in 1947. Instant photos were popular among a wide range of consumers and used for many applications, from spontaneous family snapshots, to test shots made by professional photographers, to original fine art prints created by artists. After the end of Polaroid’s instant film production in 2008, The Impossible Project began the manufacture of instant photo products in 2010, but in limited, boutique numbers.

Monochrome Print Layers

Photo emulsion is composed of a light-sensitive image material (e.g. silver particles) dispersed in a binder substance (e.g. gelatin). The emulsion is carried on a paper or sometimes a plastic support. Monochrome photographic prints are split into the following three categories popularized by James Reilly: 1-layer (image material only), 2-layer (image material in a binder), or 3-layer (image material in a binder atop a baryta coating).

Card Mounts

"Card photograph" broadly refers to any of the standard-sized commercially produced photograph card mounts of the 19th century. Of these secondary supports, the carte-de-visite, cabinet card, and stereocard are the most common. Other card photographs include the Trilby (2" × 2 ¾"), Victoria (5" × 3¼"), Promenade (7" × 4"), Boudoir (8½" × 5¼"), and Imperial (7" × 10").

Carte-de-visite

A very popular form of portraiture display, the carte-de-visite most often contained an albumen print, though occasionally a carbon print, of nearly the same size. The carte-de-visite is considered a relatively small-sized card photograph. The paper cardstock mount measures approximately 4 ¼" × 2 ½" (11.4 × 6.4 cm). Usually full-length studio portraiture, carte-de-visites were collected in albums; celebrities’ portraits, scenic views, and art reproductions were common subjects. The back print will typically bear the photographer’s name and studio address. Introduced in the late 1850s, cartes-de-visite were produced in the millions during the 1860s. By 1880 they were less common than the cabinet card but were sometimes used to display a silver gelatin print.

Cabinet card

Typically a form of portraiture display, the mid-sized cabinet photograph was larger in scale than the carte-de-visite, which predated it. A cabinet card was a thick paper cardstock measuring about 6 ¼" × 4 ¼" (15.9 × 10.8 cm) usually framing an albumen print (occasionally later forms could be silver gelatin, collodion, platinum, or carbon print) measuring about 5 ½" × 4" (13.9 × 10 cm). The lower border of the card usually bore the photographer’s name or studio insignia, and occasionally the subject’s name. The cabinet photo display was popular from 1868 through the 1890s, as scenic view postcards and enlarged studio portraits rose in public favor.

Stereocard

A stereocard photograph (synonyms: stereoview; stereograph) is intended to create three-dimensional images with the illusion of depth by emulating the stereoscopic nature of human vision. Stereocards are made up of two images, produced using a special camera with two lenses eye-width apart. This results in two images that are almost identical but are taken from slightly different viewpoints—approximating the different viewpoints seen from the left and right eyes. The images were mounted on a card (3–4½" × 7") next to one another. Cards could be viewed using a special hand-held viewer (stereoscope) that forced each eye to see only the image associated with it. Stereocards were popular from the 1850s to 1920s. The earliest stereographic images were daguerreotypes, but stereocards prior to the 1880s were usually albumen prints. Gelatin prints were dominant after the 1880s. Some stereo-pictures, printed on very thin paper, that were hand-colored (with water colors or aniline dyes) on front and back were meant for viewing through light, were called "cosmoramas." Stereocards were popular among tourists and were frequently produced by locals in vacation destinations.

Binder Materials

When we talk about the binder layer we are talking about the layer that suspends the photographically sensitive material. In the case of silver gelatin processes, gelatin binds the evenly-dispersed silver particle image into what is in total called the emulsion.

Gelatin

Gelatin is the most common binder in 20th century photo emulsions; it is a refined extract from animal wastes (i.e. bones, hoofs, hides). Because of this, gelatin will absorb moisture and accelerates chemical deterioration under humid conditions. Exposure to water and high humidity will cause gelatin to expand and soften. Due to their organic composition, gelatin emulsions are subject to biological deterioration, pest damage, and mold attack.

Gelatin blistering and frilling are two forms of nearly the same type of deterioration caused by cycling environments. Blistering happens when the gelatin emulsion bubbles or lifts from the support in localized spots; frilling occurs when the gelatin lifts in larger sections and appears to ruffle at the edges.

Collodion

Collodion is a synthetic material produced by the chemical reaction of pure cellulose with nitric acid; the collodion binder is essentially cellulose nitrate, a plastic film that is not sensitive to moisture. RH and biological deterioration are less of a concern here than with other binders. High temperature and low RH can, however, lend toward embrittlement and cracking of the binder, which is the most significant threat to a collodion emulsion. It is also very sensitive to abrasion, so much so that small scratches are often an identifying trait.

Collodion on paper is less of a factor in image deterioration than gelatin or albumen, but collodion on glass can exhibit slight blistering and frilling, caused by cycling environments. Blistering happens when the binder bubbles or lifts from the support in localized spots; frilling occurs when the binder lifts in larger sections and appears to ruffle at its edges.

Albumen

Albumen is a denatured protein: chicken egg white with salt. This solution would be evenly coated on a sheet of paper, dried, and then sensitized in a silver nitrate solution. Despite the main ingredient, albumen is not subject to pest or microorganism damage like gelatin. The primary and most obvious forms of albumen deterioration are the yellowing of image highlights and borders. Image fading will often cause shadow density to discolor toward yellow brown and lose details. Because it is denatured, albumen is not susceptible to damage as a result of moisture and humidity like gelatin. However, albumen emulsions (especially when mounted) will form tiny networks of cracks and fissures over the whole image.

Photo Paper Coatings

Regardless of the coating, paper fibers will not be fully visible, as they are obscured behind baryta (on fiber-based papers) or a whitener under a resin coating (on resin-coated papers). Before coatings were adopted (late-19th century), impurities from the paper would spread to the image and cause severe fading and discoloration. Papers coatings serve as buffers to the image material, among other things.

The earliest baryta papers may be have a thin or uneven coatings. In those cases, fibers may be evident. Resin-coated prints are easily identified by their smooth, plasticized back sides.

Baryta (1890s–present; decline after 1960s)

Description
A baryta layer is commonly found on fiber-based photo papers (as opposed to resin-coated papers). Baryta is an opaque surface coating on the paper support and below the image emulsion layer. The primary purpose of this white pigment layer (barium sulfate dispersed in gelatin) is to provide a smooth and evenly white surface over the paper fibers (though sometimes other textures were created). Additionally, baryta limits the penetration of the binder and/or image materials into the fibers and engenders more even tonalities in the image. The thicker the application of baryta, the smoother and less visible underlying paper fibers appear. The earliest papers (prior to 1900) may have a thin, uneven baryta layer or even none at all. In any case, looking at the trimmed (or dented) edges of the paper under low magnification can reveal exposed underlying fibers.
Stability
Baryta, on the whole, has proven to be stable over time. Since the 1950s, optical brightening agents have been incorporated into the whitening agent, along with a number of other additives that have proven both good and bad for the chemical stability of the image. Baryta persists–albeit slightly reformulated–used in some inkjet photo papers today.

Resin-coated (RC) paper (1968–present)

Description
Polyethylene resin-coated paper, or "RC" paper, was meant to reduce the amount of moisture absorbed by the photo paper support. RC Paper was typically used for wet process chromogenic color prints and gelatin silver prints, though also for inkjet and other digital print processes simply to imitate the look and feel of photographic papers. The benefit of RC paper was faster processing due to reduced washing and drying times. The plastic laminate was also meant to lessen print curl and drying cockle that afflicted fiber-based photo papers after wet processing. The image side layer of laminate was often pigmented white (with titanium dioxide particles) to make the image bright.
Prevalence
"[T]he rapidly increasing demand for color products outpaced all of the products and the growth of resin coated papers for many products meant that by the early 1970s about 70% of the products were resin coated rather than baryta coated" (Funderburk, 2009).
Stability
Early inclusion of titanium dioxide (as a whitener) in the RC formula led to destructive embrittlement and cracking of the emulsion side polyethylene layer (from 1968–c. 1980). Exposure to light would hasten the negative impact of this flaw. The titanium dioxide, upon exposure to light, would release oxygen. Oxygen attacked the polyethylene, making it brittle. Embrittlement coupled with fluctuating RH would then cause it to crack and flake. By the late 1970s, manufacturers worked to resolve this issue by substituting a less reactive titanium dioxide formula.

Coated peel-apart
Strictly B&W. Initially known as the Polaroid Land process, this peel-apart print requires a protective coating/stabilizer after processing. The image is pulled apart from the negative film and is subsequently hand-coated. Until 1970, all Polaroid B&W films required this protective polymer coater.
Deterioration/Flaws
All peel-apart prints have some tendency to curl in toward the image. Occasionally there will be noticeable defects at the image’s edge from an uneven spread of developer between the negative and receiving layers during processing. The peel-apart print is generally more stable than its integral counterpart. However, for prints requiring hand-coating (all B&W before 1970), prior to application, the image is extremely susceptible to damage. Once coated, the image is rather stable. If coated improperly, localized fading of the image, brownish-yellow streaking and fading may occur. Overall yellowing is also common, particularly in color photo image highlights and border.
